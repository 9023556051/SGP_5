What is PCOS?

Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) is a complex hormonal disorder that affects women of reproductive age. It's characterized by an imbalance of reproductive hormones that can wreak havoc on your reproductive system and overall health.

Key Insight

PCOS is deeply affected by the way your hormones interact with each other. When one reproductive hormone is thrown off balance, it affects how all the others work in your body, creating a cascade of symptoms and health issues.

If you have balanced hormones, your ovaries make an egg that's released into your fallopian tubes every month to await fertilization. However, with PCOS, your ovaries may not develop the egg to maturity or may not release the developed egg during ovulation.

This hormonal imbalance can cause irregular or missed periods, leading to infertility and the growth of small cysts on your ovaries. While these cysts aren't harmful on their own, they can contribute to further hormonal imbalance.

Causes of PCOS

The exact cause of PCOS is not fully understood, but several factors are believed to contribute:

Genetics

Family history plays a significant role. If your mother or sister has PCOS, you're more likely to develop it.

High Androgen Levels

Elevated levels of male hormones (androgens) can prevent the ovaries from releasing eggs normally.

Insulin Resistance

Many women with PCOS have insulin resistance, which can lead to high blood sugar and weight gain.

Common Symptoms

PCOS can manifest in various ways, and symptoms can vary significantly between individuals:

Menstrual Issues

  • Irregular or absent menstrual cycles
  • Heavy or light bleeding
  • Difficulty tracking ovulation

Weight & Metabolism

  • Difficulty managing weight
  • Insulin resistance
  • Increased risk of diabetes

Physical Changes

  • Excessive hair growth (Hirsutism)
  • Acne on face, chest, and back
  • Darkening of skin
  • Hair loss or thinning

Other Symptoms

  • Pelvic pain
  • Sleep apnea
  • Depression or mood swings
  • Multiple miscarriages

How is PCOS Diagnosed?

Important Note

The average woman with PCOS goes SEVEN YEARS without getting a proper diagnosis. This is often due to the lack of definitive tests and the need for doctors to review multiple factors.

Diagnosis typically involves several approaches:

Physical Examination

Your doctor will measure BMI, waist size, blood pressure, and check for skin discoloration, skin tags, extra hair, and thyroid enlargement.

Pelvic Examination

This exam checks for signs of excessive male hormones and determines if your ovaries are swollen or enlarged.

Pelvic Ultrasound

Using ultrasound technology, your doctor can check for cysts on your ovaries, one of the key signs of PCOS.

Blood Tests

Various blood tests check hormone levels including testosterone, prolactin, cholesterol, TSH, and glucose tolerance.

Treatment Options

Weight Management

Even a small amount of weight loss can make a significant difference. A 10% decrease in weight can help:

  • Kickstart ovulation
  • Increase chances of pregnancy
  • Normalize hormone levels
  • Improve insulin sensitivity

Dietary Changes

A balanced diet rich in fiber, lean proteins, and anti-inflammatory foods is crucial. Focus on:

  • Fiber-rich vegetables (broccoli, spinach)
  • Lean proteins (chicken, fish, legumes)
  • Anti-inflammatory foods (tomatoes, olive oil, nuts)
  • Avoid refined carbs, sugar, and processed foods

Medications

Metformin

Helps treat type 2 diabetes and can restart ovulation. Weight loss is a common side effect.

Anti-androgen Medication

Blocks the effects of androgens, helping with hair loss, excessive hair growth, and acne.

Hormonal Birth Control

Can regulate menstrual cycles, lower cancer risk, and improve acne and hair growth.

Fertility Treatment

For women trying to conceive, several options are available:

  • Fertility medication: Clomid can help increase ovulation
  • IVF: In vitro fertilization for more complex cases
  • Ovarian drilling: A surgical procedure that can restore ovulation temporarily
